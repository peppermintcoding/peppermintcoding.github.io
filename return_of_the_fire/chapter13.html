Chapter 12


"You will keep what you saw to yourself, do I make myself clear," Aelana's voice was
silent but frightening. "I will not have my secret be spread around like some folktale.
I have been hiding it successfully this whole time, and this little misstep will not
change anything about it."
"But why," Malina said. "You could do so much with your talent. You could, I don't
know, end all war?"
"Yes, I thought so too, but then I grew up and understood how this world works. If I
kill a tyrant or a war mongler all I will get as his replacement will be someone just as
cruel and selfish as the first one. And if I am extra unlucky they new Highlord will be
even worse than the one before that. No, I have lost my interest in the diplomacy
between the Highlords." Aelana was putting her weapons on the rack at the wall, her
dagger still around her waist.
An idea had blossomed in Malina's mind when she saw who Aelana really was. An
idea that could change everything. "Could you teach me then?" she asked and Aelana
froze in her movements hanging her bow on the wall.
"It is not something I could teach, especially not you."
"Why especially not me? You think I couldn't train with you?"
"It's not about that. To walk the shadows you must be trained from birth not a second
later. You could never aquire the powers I have even if I would train you for fifty
years. There is no special secret that will make you into an Onkari. And all the
masters are dead anyway."
"Wait? They are dead?"
"I do not like to dwelve in the past. Let's focus on what is ahead of us, ahead of you.
Do you know what will happen when the hunting parties get back here?"
"They will be horrified of course. An attack on their camp must be terrifiying and I
think from what I heard, we will move from here to somewhere else."
"Somewhere else? There is no somewhere else. This is as close at it gets to the border
of the forest to even have a chance of avoiding the lowlanders and getting enough
animals to hunt. There is no moving. But that is not what I am speaking about?"
"What then?" Malina asked, already afraid of what she might say.
"The camp got attacked today, while for almost two years we have been living here.
What do you think changed in this time the most?"
"They wouldn't think.. They wouldn't."
Aelana was shooting her a strange look. "I have nothing to do with this," Malina said
and sounded anxious. Could they hold her responsible. But how would she even do
something like this? Would they think she was a spy for the Lival all along and only
pretended to be a lowlander? She looked not like a forest woman for sure, but was
that enough to not direct suspicion onto her?
"Be ready for it," Aelana said. "Be ready to fight, in your case with your words
because I don't think you would have any chance in a real fight."
"What about you? Will you let them do whatever they want with me? You know I
couldn't have done this."
"Do I know that? I don't as little as all the others about you. Get up they are coming."
Malina looked helplessly at Aelana leaving the hut and she turned back and said,
"Don't worry, if you keep my secret I will get you out of this alive. I promise."
Out of this? The only place she could go from here was back home to her father. Was
that even better than dying? Malina scowled herself for the thought. Her father had
made mistakes, yes. But maybe it was time to come home anyway. He must be dying
of sorrow. Being left behind alone without any more family. The thought made
Malina sad and tears dwelled up in her eyes. Maybe it was time to get home.
She climbed down the ladder from their hut and saw the hunting parties stream back
into the camp. Their faces grim and angry. The scouts must have told them the story
about the attack already. Malina spotted Tara. Her eyes cutting through the air, Malina
saw fire in them. Like a sinbu ready to pounce and devour its prey. This would not be
easy.
The camp was in total turmoil when Malina stepped down from the ladder.
Everybody had come together and was standing around the little circular clearing in
the middle of the camp. Their faces filled with fear, anger and helplessness. Malina
was looking for Karman, to have at least somebody who might be on her side, but she
couldn't find him. He wasn't there. It felt odd. Where was he?
Choco came from the crowd and pushed some people away to get to Malina. He
looked confused and frightened. This giant of a man, frightened. It didn't help
Malina's fear.
"Are you alright?" he asked. He extended a hand to grab her by the shoulders but
froze midair when Malina slightly turned away, her body reacting before her mind
could even think a single thought.
"I am sorry," she said. "But I am alright. Everything is fine. I wasn't harmed. How
about the others?"
"It seems the children were all safe. They hid in the tree. Zaral was completely out of
it. She is frightened like the rest of us, I feel. I am glad you are alright though. The
gooddess be blessed." He kissed his extended fore and middle finger and raised them
to the sky. The rain falling heavily down on them. Their clothes were soaked already.
"I must tell you, Malina," and he moved in closer to her, his voice a whisper. "I don't
know what will happen to you. It might have been best if you left the camp already."
"So you think I am at fault as well?"
"It's not what I said. I don't believe you could even do something like this if you
wanted. You had no relations with the Lival or any other forest tribes, that was clear
at first sight. But not everybody will be thinking the same. People are frightened and
angry, we lost four of our people, slaughtered before Aelana could stop them. That
woman is a mystery to me."
"What should I do? Wouldn't leaving raise more suspicion than if I stay?"
"Surely it would, I am certain. But it's a good thing. You would be gone and
everybody could live with the thought that you were the traitor. It makes things easier
for everybody. But now that you stayed, I don't know what will happen."
Choco turned around as Karman and the two women, who were sharing meals with
Karman, Choco and Malina, walked into the middle of the clearing. Karman looked
quickly at Malina, she couldn't tell what he was thinking. His face was a mixture of
too many feelings at once. Tara, who had been walking up and down, stopped and fell
silent. Everybody fell silent one after the other. Malina spotted Zaral and the other
two kids hiding behind some of the grown ups.
"We have come together here," Karman began with a loud and clear voice, "to mourn
the dead and decide what to do with our future. The Lival have discovered our camp
and we can't be sure that they haven't sent someone back to talk of our location. We
will need to decide what to do now. I open up the discussion for everybody to take
their part. We will decide like we always do, together." The people, still silent in their
tracks, looked around for whom to go first. Malina looked at Tara and the woman
entered the circle. Karman and Tara standing face to face, encircled by the tribe's
people. Malina held her breath.
"Before we think about a new plan," Tara said, "I think it is important to talk about
the attack together. I have a simple question: how did the Lival find us here? Does
anybody have any idea?"
Karman breathed heavy. "They have their ways. The bloodhunters must have found
us by accident. I don't see any other reason for how they could find us so close to the
border."
"Oh but I can see a reason. I know you have been blinded, but a lot of us has not."
Tara turned around and a big chunk of people were nodding and shouting in
agreement. "There is no liability we have let into our tribe. A woman at that. Would
you please step forward, Malina." She spat Malina's name like it was disease. With
shaking legs she made one step into the circle, the people around her stepping back a
bit. She felt alone.
"Don't you think it is weird," Tara shouted into the tribe, "that just after a few weeks,
the Lival attacked us, at the exact moment we changed our number of guards.
Because of the scarcity of animals we had to leave fewer guards behind." Again,
nodding and agreement.
"But I didn't even know that," Malina tried to say. Her voice shaky and tears coming
to her eyes. She wasn't learned in these types of confrontations. They reminded her
too much of her father's anger.
"Of couse you didn't know that. Who would believe that. Even a blind person could
have noticed."
"That is quite enough, Tara," Karman said. He was controlled, his voice barely above
his normal magnitude. "It is not Malina's fault we were attacked. She comes from the
lowlands not as a spy of the Lival."
"Of course would you take her side. I think you can't think straight in this situation,
my friend. You have been lusting over her ever since she set foot in this camp with
that rainin' bastard Dago." She spat out on the ground. "I have told you all, I have
cautioned you all, to not get with the lowlanders. None of them. The wares are not
enough for the trouble. And we have reaped what we had sewn."
"I did not lust over her and you are simply throwing around accusations because you
are angry. I understand that you are, but this is no place for wrongful accusations.
Malina has been nothing but kind to us and has been teaching our children."
"Oh right," a man stepped forward into the circle. Malina had never noticed him
before. A long scar over his cheek, black hair, small but muscular. "What has she
been teaching them? Nothing but drawing in the sand. What does that help our
situation? Does it bring any food for us? Any safety? It was your delurious dream to
teach them, but what for?"
"You know the answer to that," Tara said. "His rainin' stone tablets. Karman, let me
ask you, when will you finally give up on them. They are worthless. Unreadable, we
have asked before and always got the same answer, or did this little girl come to a
conclusion."
All eyes were on Malina. "What I found out about the stone tablets?"
"Don't play dumb with me," Tara said. Her face a grimace of anger and disgust.
Something Malain knew all to well.
"Well," she began with shaking voice, "I have studied the stone tablets and as far as I
can see they are all about the sinbu."
"Oh about the sinbu," Tara said acting surprised, "What do they tell you?"
"I don't exactly know. But it might be a guide for taming the beasts. If I have more
time I-"
"Thank you." Tara looked around the circle. More and more were looking angry at
Malina, or was that only her anxiety changing the reality in front of her. "I think we
heard enough. Taming a wild sinbu? Nothing more than myths from the old days.
Dreamstories for children, nothing more."
"It doesn't matter what you believe if they hold truth or not," said Karman. "Malina
has no fault in this attack. We are here to discuss the attack not the value of our stone
tablets. If you don't believe, then don't. I will hold onto my own beliefs if it is alright
with you."
"But that's exactly the problem, is it not? You are living in your dream world Karman.
We need a leader who has the good of our people in his mind. Not stories of dreams
and want. We need someone strong. Someone who can make the hard decisions and
punish those who bring misfortune to our tribe."
"Careful with your words," it was Choco's voice from Malina's back. "Do you know
what you are saying?"
Malina looked around. Something was different. Their anger had made way for
something else in their faces. Something Malina didn't know what it was. Karman
looked silently at Tara. The woman walking up and down like a wild cat.
"I know what I am saying. Not what you thought, Choco, I do not want to challenge
for chieftain, I want to have certainty. If those stone tablets are true and there is a way
to tame the wild beasts, than why don't we try it. Or why don't you try it?" She was
facing Malina now. Her heart stopped. She had barely decyphered the a couple of
symbols and was rather sure it was about taming the sinbu, but actually doing it
would require more knowledge, something she didn't know she even could aquire
with years of study. A hunch told her, Tara wasn't willing to give her this amount of
time.
"We haven't gotten to the whole truth of the tablets yet," Karman said. "This is
ludicrous. I will not stand for this. I will not let her throw her life away so you can get
your anger out on somebody."
"But it's not your decision to make. We all have a say in this. I propose a deal: if
Malina will be able to tame a sinbu and bring them back into the camp, I will be the
first one to forgive her, for being wrong about her. But if she is a traitor, like it is
obvious, she will find her given punishment."
This couldn't be true. How was Malina suppose to get behind the truths of the stone
tablets? Malina looked around the circle of people. A couple of dozen strangers,
really nothing more, deciding her fate.
"Alright, we put it towards a vote," Karman said. "If you believe Malina should be
tested like Tara suggested, which is I might add, ludicrous, raise your fist to the sky
and let your voice be heard. The majority will decide."
All at once a good chunk of people raised their hands to the sky, so fast it looked like
they waited for this moment the whole time. Malina was taken aback. It was almost
half of them. Malina looked back at Choco, having his arms firm beside his sides and
looking grave at his tribe's men and women. Karman looked around as well, seeing
what his people would decide. Another couple of people raising their arms. Slowly
the tides turned and Malina thought it was the end.
"Stop it," a voice shouted from behind the ranks. The voice of a child, and Malina's
heart began to race. "Let Malina alone, she has done nothing bad. I can prove it."
Everybody stopped in their tracks, the little girl came through the closed cirlce of
people. She looked so small, so fragile. But even then she had more guts and courage
than Malina could ever muster up. Slowly some of the people lowered their hands,
confused of why this child interrupted their gathering.
"It is noble of you to come forward," Tara said without the hate and anger in her
voice. It felt more soft while she spoke to Zaral. And then it struck Malina, there was
something familiar in Tara's face, she found so often in Zaral. Could it be? "But this
is a decision you cannot take part in. You are a child, you don't have the mind for
judgement in this situation."
Zaral ignoring her and speaking with a loud and clear voice, "When the Lival raided
our camp, Mon, Ezir and me, fled with Malina. They were on our tracks and we
climbed up a tree to hide, the tree Aelana would come to get us when it was over.
Malina couldn't get up the tree, because she slippe while climbing, but instead of
showing the Lival where we were, she ran away. Practically giving up her life to keep
us saafe and unseen. She got the attention of the Lival and lured them away. Without
her, we could be dead. How can you doubt a person like that?"
"I think you had a hard day, Zaral," Tara said, a little less soft. "But you are a child
and this is a gathering for men and women, not children. For all we know, you could
be lying because you don't want your new friend to suffer punishment. Your
judgement is clearly clowded."
Zaral gave Tara a vile glance, so much anger in this little girl. All Malina wanted to
do was to go to her and hug her, tell her it's going to be fine. This was all a
misunderstanding. But the people around her started nodding towards what Tara had
said to them.
"I think her statement is valid," Karman said, "and shall be taken into account. This
decision should be made with all the facts we have. I want to stress again," he paused
for a second, "that Malina comes from the low lands and she had nothing to do with
the tribe of the Lival, living near the center of the forest. How could she made contact
in anyway?"
"You say she comes from the lowlands," Tara spat again, "but take a look at her. Have
you ever seen a woman of the lowlands give up their rainin' neck protection? She
seemed awfully quick to take off that piece of clothing, something deemed to stay
there by law of their god. I don't see a real lowlander, I see a pretender."
Again, nodding and shouts of encouragement. Malina took what little courage she
had and spoke, "I only changed my way of clothing to fit better with you people. I
want to be a part of this tribe, so I left my old customs behind me. All I wanted was to
fit in, not to be seen as the outsider I am."
"You will never fit in here," Tara said.
"That's enough," Karman said. "Let's put it to a vote again. Everybody in favor of
Tara's proposal, raise your arms to the sky and let your voices be heard."
The vote didn't go in Malina'a favor and she felt bad for the little Zaral, who had
spoken up for her but had to watch her words to be unnoticed. Karman showed her to
follow him to his hut and she did, small tears running down her face mixing with the
rain streaming down from above.
Karman didn't talk when Malina entered the hut. He sat on the floor with his head in
his hands. Malina sat down opposite of him. The water from the rain still dripping
down to the ground.
"Apologies Malina," Karman finally said. "I couldn't protect you. I don't know how I
could be so optimistic at first."
"What do you mean?" she asked.
"When I first saw you with Dago coming to our camp, I thought you were the most
beautiful woman I had ever seen. I thought if you were here for a couple of months,
sharing food with us, learning our ways, that you and me, that we could, someday.
But it didn't work out that way I fear."
Malina was taken aback by this forward and honest sharing of his feelings for her.
She never heard anyone talk about her this way. The only one confessing his love to
her was a servant boy when she was fifteen, but the boy quickly left their service after
Malina's father took notice of his behaviour and dismissed him from their house.
"Apologies, but I haven't come here to be someone's wife," Malina said a little too
directly. "I mean, I feel flattered by your compliments, but I didn't plan on taking a
husband any time soon. Apologies, again."
"I understand," he said and stroked his chin. "Then there is only one more question to
answer, when will you leave to go back to your father?"
Malina was ready for the question, she had played it back in her mind a thousand
times. Going back, was a good idea. Was a bad idea? She didn't know because
everytime she asked herself, the result was different. She wasn't sure at all. "I
honestly don't know. I don't want to leave."
"But you can't stay. You haven't gotten to the secret yet."
"That's what I fear as well. Maybe there is something there, maybe something that
brings me back into grace with the others."
"Even if you decypher the whole secret, I will not let you go and test them yourself. I
myself will go and see if you were right or wrong?"
"How can you trust me so much with this." Karman fell silent and thought. Malina's
legs were hurting and she adjusted herself.
"It's not that," he said slowly. "I have been losing the status of chieftain slowly but
surely over the last year. My people don't believe I can get them out of this mess. We
are rivals to the Lival because of me. Because I made a mistake and the only way I
see to get our old lives back is to get behind these stone tablets. If we could tame the
sinbus, we could march back to the center and reclaim our status. But without them,
we have nothing. We will fade slowly, die of hunger maybe. A slow and painful death
I tell you, and they will not stand for it. If they need someone to put the blame on,
they found you, but after that I will be the next to fall." He paused again. "I need your
help, Malina."
"How long can you give me?" she asked.
"Maybe a week, probably not much longer. You seriously want to stay and try, rather
than go back to your father's?"
"I don't know what I want, but right now it feels right for me to decide to help you as
best as I can. I cannot leave you after all you have done for me. Taking me in, giving
me a home even if it was temporary and at times felt nothing like one. I am willing to
help and get behind the secret. And when the time comes, we will test our theory
together. Either we emerge together or die trying."
