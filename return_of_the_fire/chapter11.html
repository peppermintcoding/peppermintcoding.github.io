Chapter 10


"Aelana, why is it that so many people here are suspicious of me?" The two women
were getting ready for the day, washing off the night and clothing themselves. Malina
wore for the first day, her new garments. She decided to expose her neck and not
cover it by unnecessary cloth. She wore tight pants with iron beads knitted into the
fabric, that didn't hang on to every little branch that stuck out from a tree or bush.
Over it a short sleeve shirt made out of leather and fur. It looked good on her, Malina
decided. The cloth hugging her body, making her feel exposed but confident. She
touched her naked neck and felt shame rising from inside, but she fought it back. This
was going to be the first day of her new life. She would embrace it and not dwelve in
the past too much.
"They don't like lowlanders. They bring trouble." Aelana put on her boots and hid two
knives in them.
"I heard that before," Malina said. "But why is that? What kind of trouble would I
bring them? I couldn't hurt anybody here, even if I wanted. I didn't learn to fight or
kill. I learned to be a lady, that is of no use here and of no danger to others."
"It is not your ability to kill they fear. Maybe fear is a strong word for it anyway. The
first forest people might have been lowlanders, too, fleeing from their lands into the
arms of the trees. But that has been hundreds, probably even thousands of years. To
them you are a total stranger, still."
"So what can I do to change that?"
"Well, you done well with wearing something more appropriate for this enviroment,
but it won't be enough. You would need to start a relationship with someone here,
most likely. But even then it could take years before they accept you as one of them."
"They seem to accept you alright, even without courting a man."
"Right. I am different." She grabbed her bow and a couple of arrows from the rack. "I
bring back food. More food than any other hunting group, that's why they don't
bother me and I don't bother them. We live together because it is safer. If that safety
ends, I am gone as fast as a sunny day."
"You are bluntly honest, I see."
"Always have."
"So then tell me, what's your story. I know nothing about you. Only things I know is
you can hunt and are a lowlander like me."
"Right, and that's all you need to know and all you will ever know about you. I need
to get going."
"Why go early if you won't need the whole day for hunting?"
"I like the mornings for hunting, that's all." Aelana left the hut through the only
entrance there was. A dagger hanging on her side, sheathed in black leather with gold
and iron engravings on it. As far as Malina remembered, it was the only weapon
Aelana had never hung on the rack or left alone in the hut. Seemed to be something
with emotional value to her. Something from her past. But all Malina could do was
speculate. The woman was like a mud wall when it came to conversation. She would
definitely not be fun at a dinner party.
The familiar rain greeted Malina when she left the hut herself, falling softly on her
skin and head. The sound was soothing and refreshing. Sleeping on the hard floor was
not easy to get used to, but now she felt more refreshed than she had ever before.
Like in her old life, something had been suffocating her to the point where she was
unable to breathe deeply. The air felt fresh and clean while she breathed with her
whole body, soaking in the energy.
Lately, Malina was eating her breakfast alone. Karman and Choco had explained to
her, that the forest wasn't providing in a way it once did. So far from the center of it,
it was even worse. They didn't tell her anything about why they were living so far to
the corner of the forest and Malina understood that it was a topic neither of the tribes
people wanted to talk to her about. Fruits, nuts and other foraging food was hard to
get and so they were forced to hunt more than prior. But hunting was going slowly,
deer were thinning in the forest and smaller animals weren't enough for a couple of
dozen people. So they ate their food on the way to their hunting grounds, leaving only
the children, some guards, Ruse and Malina herself at the camp. It was the only way
they could assure the survival of the tribe.
Luckily her teaching went better with every day. Zaral had persuaded the other two
children, Ezir and Mon, to join them for writing and the children learned quickly.
Zaral was the fastest of them, always asking questions about everything. The others
were a bit more reserved but Malina could tell from their developing skills, they were
listening to everything.
Today Malina decided, to teach the children more about how to write down traditions
and customs of their people. Karman had asked her to do so, in order for them to have
something to look back on. How festivals were celebrated, or how hunting was done,
how the old were honored and how the gods were praised. Malina learned a lot
herself during that time, about what made these people so different from her own or
herself.
"What do we learn today," Zaral ask eagerly. She couldn't stay still and jumped up
and down next to Malina, pulling on her sleeves. Ezir with his deep blue eyes which
made him look so much more mysterious than he actually was. He felt more, simple,
to Malina. Mon on the other hand was kind of indifferent about it. She was a year
younger than Zaral, but instead of putting her heart into learning, she spend most of
her time complaining about that this won't bring food for the tribe and she would
rather learn something more important. She did participate though, which didn't make
much sense for Malina. Why be so negative but still participate?
"We will learn some new words today," Malina said. They were standing under a
selfmade leaf roof. The children had build after the rain had started again. It kept the
four people dry and didn't wash away their markings. "I thought about festives today.
What kind of festives do you celebrate? We will then try to write down the words
necessary to describe these things?"
"Why would we want to do that?" Mon asked with her arms crossed in front of her
chest. Her dark hair braided with iron rings in them. "We already know how they are
celebrated. Why spend time writing something what everybody knows already?"
"That's a good question. Maybe you know if, and maybe everybody else knows it
right now, but in the future they might not. Maybe there will be a discussion about
how things used to be and then you can go back and read what was written these
days. So there is no room for any differing opinions. Written words don't change, how
people see the world can change. Our memories sometimes play tricks on you."
The memory hit her hard in the stomach. The memory of her father. Was she
remembering things differently too? Was he actually a good man? Or did she
remember him better than he actually was? She felt he was a different man back in
the days when Malina was younger. She always thought the dreamshrooms and her
mother's death changed him, but then again did it? Malina remembered her father
hitting her as a child when she was crying. She had no idea why she was crying and
no idea why he hit her, but it was vivid and hurtful. It happened often. Her father.
"Malina?" Zaral asked concerned. "What is it?"
"Oh nothing," Malina answered. "Where were we? Ah yes." She put these memories
as far back in her head as she could. He might have been unfair but he was a good
man. The problem were these rainin' shrooms. She would find a way to change that,
find a way to get her father off these poisonous things.
Malina was about to introduce to the kids the symbol for god, when a horn cut
through the constant rain background noise. It was long and deep. The children froze
in their actions and looked to where the sound was coming. Malina had no idea was
going on.
"What is-" but Malina couldn't finish her question.
"We need to get back," Zaral shouted and grabbed Ezir and Mon with her hands and
dragged them back to the camp. Malina followed unsure what was happening.
One of the guards, a tall young man with a short boy and a sword at his side, black
hair and long hair, was waiting for them.
"They found us," he shouted towards them. "You have to run. Go south to the border,
they won't expect you to run that way. We will hold them off. Cover your tracks as
best you can."
"What is happening?" Malina asked but the man was already off, running towards
where the horn had been blown. He ran fast, water splattering behind him.
The children ran off, south where Malina had come from.
Zaral looked back, "Come on Malina, the Lival tribe has found us. We need to go. We
can't fight them without Karman and the others. Come on, hurry." And Malina did.
She didn't know what was going on, but this child seemed to know what to do. So she
followed them south, away from her new home.
While running the kids tried to cover their tracks with walking on fallen trees and
stumps to not leave a mark and Malina did her best to do the same, she slipped and
almost fell, but she found her balance just at the precarious time. The children were
fast and Malina had difficulty keeping up. Her body wasn't made for this. She never
had to run anywhere, but as a small child maybe. But even then she did other things
than play in the woods like these children. It was diheartening to know that little
children were better at something than herself.
Zaral stopped and told everybody they should hold and breathe for a few minutes.
The others did as they were told. This seemed to be not the first time this had
happened.
"Who are these Lival," Malina asked.
"It was a neighbouring tribe," Zaral explained, "when were living deeper in the forest.
We were good friends once but things changed and now they declared war on us."
"For what?"
"Because Karman is an idiot," Mon said, her eyes furious. "If it wasn't for him
everything would be fine and we wouldn't live in exile near the border."
"Let's not fight and keep going," said Zaral, who seemed to understand the anger. She
looked at Mon with a certain look, Malina wasn't able to read herself.
The sun was almost at its peak and Malina was thinking about Aelana. This was
normally the time when she would come back from her hunting. Would she encounter
the Lival and fight them? She probably would run away, go on and live with someone
else, like she put it.
Another horn sung close to Malina, towards the camp.
"What does that mean?" she asked and saw the terrified faces of the three children.
"They found our trail. We need to hide," Zaral's voice not more than a whisper, her
eyes as wide as her face.
"Where would we hide," Malin asked but the kids were alreay climbing one of the
trees. Swiftly and without apparent effort the three children vanished in the canopy of
the tree.
"What are you rainin' waiting for," Zaral whispered from above. "Come on."
Malina climbed up the tree with the help of a lower hanging branch. She put the feet
towards the trunk and slowly made her way up the tree. Her arms were shivering and
her breath already fast and shallow. Her legs hurt from the running and she was
losing grip on the slippery bark. She knew what would happen before it actually
happened. Malina was slipping on the bark, her arms unable to hold her weight and
she lost hold of the branch as well. With a hard landing she fell to the ground, the
bones in her bottom hurt. He hands full of mud and leaves. Like this she would never
be able to climb that tree. She wouldn't find a hold.
And there she saw them. A group of five men running through the woods. Still quite
some way away but close enough to see them shine between the trees. All were
holding bows with an arrow already on the bowstring. Was this how it all would end.
Malina made a quick decision and jumped up from where she sat, shouted as loud as
she could and ran off further south. Branches cutting her cheeks, she jumped over
fallen trees, ferns almost brought her down and the damp and soft ground underneath
swallowed her steps. She didn't look back. All she could hope for, was to lure the men
away from the three children. At least she could do that.
A faint breeze blew over her face. Malina looked back saw the men so close to her,
she could already see their faces. She held her breath. This was the end. Two of them
drew their bows. She could dodge behind a tree, for sure. But what would it matter?
A shemen appeared behind one of the men, who had drawn his bow, a shemen with a
Sinbu fur on their shoulder, the iron blade cut through the man's throat and then it
vanished. It happened so fast. When the first of the five men touched the floor, his
throat full of crimson colored blood, the shemen cut the last one's throat just like the
others. The men fell to their deaths in a quick succession like there were strings
attached to them and if one fell the others were destined to fall themselves.
And there Malina saw Aelana stand over the last of the five men, her blade dripping
with blood, her gaze fixed on Malina.
A single word escaped her mouth. Not more than a whisper, a breeze of air maybe,
"Onkari."
