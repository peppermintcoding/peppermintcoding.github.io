Chapter 8


Malin woke from her sleep with a hand pressed hard on her lips. Unable to make a
sound. Still dizzy from the sleep she tried to find out what was going on.
"Don't start screaming if I let go of your mouth, understand?", a familar voice
whispered in her ear.
Malina nodded slowly and the hand left her mouth. "What were you doing this for",
Malina asked.
"You were screaming in your sleep. I didn't want the whole camp to lose their sleep
because you having a nightmare."
"Oh." She felt embarassed. Aelana was getting up beside her, the light barely enough
to see arm lengths in front of you. The rain had stopped.
"You should go to the seamstress today and get yourself some clothing fit for the
forest. With this tattered dress you will soon walk around naked." Aelana still sitting
on her side of the small hut, she picked up a wodden item. Malina had never seen
anything therelike. It was long and narrow, with a ball at one end. The ball had an
openeing at the top where it was black like the night. The rest was of a shallow
brown.
"What is that", she asked and then added quickly, "If I may ask you a question."
"Here is a little tip for you", she put dried leaves into the ball at the end, "If you want
to survive and be fully acknowledged in this tribe, you need to act differently. Don't
ask for permission to ask a question. Don't stare at people just because they are
clothed differently than you. It's no crime to show some skin, even if it is your neck
or breast."
"Well, why don't you dress like the others", Malina responded. "I don't see you blend
well with the other people here."
Aelana brought her tinder to ignite with a wodded drill and lit the dry leaves inside.
"We both might not dress like the others but we are not equals in this. I made a
decision to dress the way I do. I see the custom and I acted against them because I
wanted to. You don't decide to wear that piece of cloth around your neck. You accept
the things like they are and don't try to change them at all. Not that there is ever a
way to change things in this world, but that's beside the point." Aelana inhaled with
the narrow piece between her lips and let out a big cloud of smoke. "It's a pipe, by the
way."
"I can make my own decisions", she said, her voice shaky. "You have no idea what I
am capable of."
"I don't? Well, why don't you proof me wrong than. Actions speak louder than
words."
"What if I choose to keep wearing something to cover my neck? You took away my
choice in this. If I take your advice and change my clothing the way the others wear
it, I would still simply follow a norm and not from my own choosing. If I don't
change anything, I keep with the old. Is there even a choice to make, that is my own
here?"
Aelana laughed out loud and choked a bit on her smoke. "You are not as simple
minded as I thought you be. Keep surprising me and I will actually want to remember
your name."
"Why wouldn't you want to remember my name?"
She looked Malina straight in the eye. "You are not the first lowlander to seek exile in
these woods. I am guilty of it myself. The only difference is, I know how to take care
of myself. The others, not so much. So they either leave and crawl back to where they
came from or they die quickly. Some because they can't hunt or forage enough, so the
tribe will not feed them through, or because they get eaten by some vile creature. It's
always the same. Nothing personal."
Malina couldn't provide for herself. How could she? She was raised to be a lady who
goes on balls and banquets. She was never to live alone without a father or husband.
But she made a deal with Karman. She just needs to work on those stone tablets in
exchange for shelter and food. Wasn't that the deal?
"I made a deal with Karman", she said. "He wanted me to tell him what was written
on the stone tablets. I won't need to go hunting to provide for myself."
"The only way to provide for yourself without going into the woods is with your legs
spread wide open and a fruitfull belly. Don't even think for a minute you can read
what was written on those rainin' tablets. He is holding on to some old myth. Nobody
can read them anymore. Or maybe noone ever could."
"Apologies, but what did you say to me? I will not use my body and spirit to pay for
my food and shelter. Is that what you think of me?"
"I don't think about you at all, little bird. I will go now, hunting is always done best in
the morning." Aelana picked her bow and the arrows from rack of weapons and made
it out the opening.
Her words gave Malina food for thought. Was it Karman's secret desire to get her to
share his bed with him? Was he looking that way at her? She couldn't tell, she never
could. A servant boy named Erin was once trying to get Malina's attention in that
way. She didn't notice until her father chased him out of their house. He was always
kind to her, looked her straight in the eyes with a bright smile. Wasn't Karman doing
the same?
No. She needed to stay focussed. Getting answers from the tablets would be enough
for her to stay here. Where else would she go anyway?
Crawling out of the hut, sun rays painting the forest in a warm light, Malina looked
around her new found home. People were going about their daily business. Fire were
ignited for cooking the breakfast, children running around and playing games. A
couple of birds singing in the background.
She made her way down from their tree home, down the wanky ladder, swaying little
by little from side to side, but she made it safe down anyway. Finally with her boots
on the ground she felt more safe and made her way towards Karman's eating circle.
Like yesterday, the two woman and the shaven giant Choco were sitting by his side.
One spot empty, for herself she hoped.
"Do you mind if I join you?", she asked.
"We were just waiting for you to arrive", Choco said with his deep voice. "How was
your first night in this mysterious forest? Anything like sleeping at home?"
"It was quite different from my former home." She tried to stress 'former'. "But it was
quite alright, I feel refreshed and ready."
Two men were bringing food to all of the eating circles, different ones than yesterday
evening. Maybe they were rotating the responsibility or there were different people
responsible for different meals.
"I wish to talk with you about your work here", Karman said while he chewed on a
piece of meat. Back at her father's house this would be unheard of. Speaking with a
full mouth, a social suicide. "I want to extend your responsibility. I want you to teach
our children to draw words and read them back. We discussed it yesterday eve and
came to the conclusion it could be worthwhile for us."
"So I will split my time from working with the tablets and teaching your children
then?"
"All children will come to you to learn during the early morning. After midday they
will go about something else and you will have enough time to work on the tablets."
"I guess you can't wait to finally know what they are saying, can you?", Choco asked.
"I have waited long enough I think", Karman answered. "They hold answeres to a
mystery older than we are, I know it. I lay all my hopes in your hands, Malina."
Malina felt a lot hotter than she should have. The sun rays were bright but it was early
morning. Her heart thumping in her chest. She was good at writing and reading, she
read dozens of books from her father's library, but she didn't know if the tablets were
written with the same system nor with the same language they were speaking.
"I will do my very best to uphold your trust in me", she said to Karman and finally
began to eat her breakfast. Besides meat there were berries again and an asortment of
nuts and seeds. It dawned on her, that the food in the forest wasn't as diverse as back
in Nigita. "How many children will I have for teaching?"
"Three. The others are too small to learn yet or old enough to learn hunting and
foraging. Gathering food will always have priority."
The breakfast was done faster than the evemeals and there was no singing or loud
converstation. Everybody was getting ready to leave the camp for the day and hunt in
the woods. Malina made her way to Karman's hut, where the children should be
waiting for her. When she finally climbed up the ladder, nobody was there. Malina
started to panick. What if the children didn't want her to teach them? How should she
act then? Force the children? But how so?
She climbed back down, the camp already almost empty. Two men and a woman
always stayed behind to guard the camp, they couldn' afford to leave more. They
were climbing up the trees into their canopy or walking in a big circle around the
camp, watching out for strangers. A woman was sitting outside with pieces of leather
and cloth around her. She must be the seamstress. Malina walked over the clearing to
talk to her.
"Aplogies for interrupting your work", Malina said. "I am Malina."
"Oh I know who you are", the woman smiled at her. A bone needle in one hand and a
thread in the other. Her hair was long and in braids with iron rings woven in between.
Her clothing like a rainbow with all colors you could imagine. "My name is Ruse. I
suppose you have come to change these hideous clothes into something more, well,
fitting?"
Malina blushed. Hideous clothes? "No, no. I", she startled. Maybe she should get
some new clothes. Her dress was ripped and tattered from the thicket she walked
through and she felt horribly out of place already. Her clothes weren't doing her any
favors with that. "If I were to want new clothes, what would I have to do for them?"
"Well, my dear, you have to ask me nicely. I will do the rest don't you worry."
"Apologies, but I don't seem to understand. I don't have to pay you anything for the
clothes?". Malina was baffled. Clothes were hard and expensive to make, especially
in this forest were the next fabric store was days away.
"With what would you pay me anyway", she asked and laughed. "We have no money
here. Everybody provides something for the tribe. I make clothes. Karman is our
chief and your sleeping buddy, Aelana, is our best hunter. We all pull our weight. You
will, as I heard, be a teacher for the little ones."
"Yes, I will." What a weird concept. No money? "Only problem is I can't seem to find
the three little ones."
"They are most likely hiding from you. They would rather go on with the adults for
the hunt. If you follow the sun, you should get to a small creak. The little ones often
play there if they don't want to be found."
"My thanks, Ruse", Malina said. "And I would wish some new clothes. But can I ask
for a few alterations?"
"Don't worry, I know how you lowlanders like to dress, my dear. I will see to it."
Malina hesitated. She could just leave it like this. Not mention that she would like to
have her neck exposed, but that would defeat the purpose. "If it isn't any trouble, I
would like my neck to be exposed, but I would want something to cover my chest
completely."
"Ah, you are already starting to embrace your new life. It's a big step I am sure. I will
get right to it. Maybe I even have some pieces for you already."
"Don't you need to get my measurements first? How do you know they will fit me?"
"I am doing this my whole life. Don't you worry, I have keen eyes."
So Malina thanked the seamstress again and made her way to the little creak, where
the children were supposedly hiding from her. How would she convince them to
come with her?
The sun was blinding her as Malina made her way through the forest. It was a rare
treat to see and feel the sun in all of its glory. The Enlightening must be sending her a
sign, that this was a good idea. He was offering her His support. The trees were far
apart and she could see for quite some distance. The leaves shone in a bright green
with the sun behind them, the air felt warm. After a little while Malina could hear the
creak gurgle ahead of her. It was like a thin blue snake carving throught he forest. The
water level was lower than when it rained, so around the water was a vegetation free
zone. The children were nowhere in sight.
What did she think? They would be right here, where she happened upon the water?
They could be either down stream or upstream for quite some way. How was she
supposed to know? Anxiety rising in her stomach. What would Karman do if she
couldn't teach the kids or even worse not decypher the tablets? She didn't want to
think about that.
"Hello", she said, but scowlded herself for it. She needed to be louder than this for
anybody to hear her. "Hello", she tried again, still barely above her previous one.
"What do you want." Malina startled and jumped. Noone around her, but she heard
the voice. The voice of a girl.
"Where are you", Malina asked and turned around searching for the speaker.
"Up here", the voice said. Malina turned and looked up. In one of the trees, a little
girl, not older than six years, was sitting comfortably with her back against the trunk
and her legs dangling at either side of a thick branch. Her face was smeared with
mud, her clothes just the same.
"Hello", Malina tried again, "I am Malina. I am new here but Karman has instructed
me to teach you how to write and read words. But for that I need you to come down
from that tree."
"But I don't want to come down. Why don't you come up here. We can learn just as
good here as down there."
"That's a terrible idea. Where would we write on, where would we get something to
write on in the first place." Malina began to panic. She didn't even think about on
what she would write with these children. She only ever wrote on nice pieces of
paper. But there was no paper here.
"If we can't do it up here, I don't want to", the girl said.
Malina tried to calm herself. She would find a way to get that dirty child down from
the tree. "Why don't we start with our names. I am Malina. What's your name?"
"Zaral", the girl mumbled. "Is it true you come from outside the forest?"
"Yes, it is. I am from Nigita."
"You know, I actually wanted to stay and learn from you. But Ezir and Mon said they
didn't want to." She leaned closer, Malina feared she could fall from the tree. "They
don't like you very much."
"Why would they not like me? They don't know me yet."
"They know you are a lowlander", Zaral said and began to climb down from her tree.
"They are younger than me and they can't think for themselves yet. The adults were
talking about you and they listened on. It's nothing personal, you know." Why had
nobody talked to her directly? Malina thought they accepted her. The only one
speaking up was Tara.
The last part of the tree, Zaral jumped off and landed safely on her feet. "Where are
the other two", Malina asked and tried not to sound hurt.
"They are over there", Zaral pointed over the creak and up a tree. Behind the cover of
the leaves, Malina saw two small children hanging in the tree. She tried talking to
them but she didn't get a single word out from them. She was devastated. She had no
idea children could be that difficult. Malina herself would have never acted this way.
If she ever spoke against her father or her tutor, she had been hit several times.
Malina didn't remember how her mother treated these things. She was too young
when her mother died.
"Is there any way I can convince them to come down, Zaral", she asked.
"No", she answered. She was crouching down and playing with a little bug walking
through the sand and clay ground. It was still wet from the reciding creak.
"Alright, fine", Malina said and looked at the children hiding in the tree. "We will
begin without you two, but you can come down whenever you want and join us." She
was hoping for them to get curious enough to want to come and down from their
hiding place. Otherwise she would only have one pupil after all.
Malina looked for a long twig, with which she could write in the sandy ground near
the creak. She would need to make the symbols big for them to be visibile from up
the tree.
"So, what would you like to learn how to write", Malina asked.
"I don't know", Zaral answered looking up from the ground.
"Why don't we start with your name. Zaral is a beautiful name. It consist of two parts.
The zar symbol and the ral symbol. I will draw them into the sand here and you will
try to do it like me afterwards, alright?"
Zaral nodded and Malina began to draw the symbols. It was harder than she expected.
With ink and paper the lines, curves and dots were easy to get down clean. But with
little stones in the way here, she her lines were crooked, the curves not smooth at all.
It wasn't hard for Malina to remember the symbols at all. She had memorized them
perfectly. A short moment of proudness for her.
"How do you know where the lines go?", Zaral asked after she finished. She was
walking around them looking at them from different angles.
"Someone told me where they go for each of the parts."
"But there are so many words. You know how to draw every single one of them?"
Malina thought for a second, "Probably most of them. I would have to look special
ones up in a book, but they are not used frequently anyway."
"Can I try now?", Zaral asked.
"Sure. Do it right next to mine. Do it slowly." Zaral made her first line with lots of
care, slowly but precisely and jumped back to Malina's first symbol to see where the
next one would go. The symbols were a good step long and high.
Malina looked over to the tree where the children were hiding, but they didn't move
and showed no intention to do so. They had to come down sometime though. Malina
was impressed by how well Zaral drew the symbols. Her strokes were almost cleaner
than her ones and the child's eyes were wide, shining with a deep brown. A smile on
her face, she began the second symbol, faster this time.
"Very impressive", Malina said after the girl had finished. And she meant it.
"What do we learn next?", she asked.
"First we need to clean the ground to have a nice canvas to write on again", Malina
said. The girl grabbed a piece of bark and began to fill in the lines with new sand.
Malina showed Zaral all sorts of symbols, from the symbol for tree, man, woman and
the sun to more advanced symbols like water or river. Some of the words were
written with a single symbol. In that case it often had some resemblance to the actual
word. In other cases there was no single symbol for it and so you had to write the
word with their syllables next to each other. Sadly, the other two never joined Malina
and Zaral, which made Malina very uncomfortable. How would she explain that to
Karman?
As the sun rose to its highest point in the clear blue sky, Malina and Zaral made their
way back to the camp. Zaral fell behind her to catch up with the other two kids who
followed Malina with quite some distance between them. She didn't want to confront
them after the failed attempt in getting them to learn from her. What did she think
would happen? What did Karman thought would happen here? She wasn't a teacher,
she didn't know these people at all and now she was suppose to tell the children what
to do. It was prone to fail at the start.
Zaral came running back to Malina, "Can I ask you something?" Her eyes were clear
and strong surrounded by the mud and dirt on her face.
"Of course you can", Malina answered and hoped it was writing related. "Anything.
What is it you want to know?"
"Ezir didn't want to ask you himself, he is a bit shy. But I think we were all this way
when we were younger. I sure think I was. But now I am a lot more confident. My
father always says I am noisy, but my mum hits him for that, so I don't really know."
"I thought you had a question", Malina interrupted by started giggling. The girl was
adorable.
"Yes, yes. He wants to know why your clothes go up to your head. He thinks it looks
stupid. And isn't it too warm under there?"
"Well, where I come from, women are expected to hide their neck from people who
are not their family. It's a little bit like women who grew up here show one of their
breasts. Well, it's something like that."
"Is it? I don't understand why you hiding something of your body and women here
showing part of their body is similar. But don't worry, Ezir doesn't know what he is
talking about. He is just a little baby who can already walk. I think you look beautiful
even with this thing around your neck."
"I guess, thank you. I think that was a compliment."
As they entered the round clearing in the middle of the camp, Aelana was already
back from her hunt. She had several rabbits tied to a branch and was working on
skinning one of them already. The smell of blood and fresh meat hung in the air like
soft mist. How did she manage to be back so much faster than the other? And why
was she always going alone?
Enough with the useless questioning, Malina told herself. She needed to concentrate.
Teaching the children wasn't a success, but it was probably a mere job to keep her
occupied in the eyes of the other tribe members. Her real job was working out the
stone tablets, for which Karman seemed to have high hopes.
Malina grabbed something to eat while she worked, said goodbye to the smiling
Zaral, who ran of with the other kids, and made her way to Karman's hut. The stone
tablets never left his hut and she was instructed to do her work inside, sheltered from
the eyes of the other tribe's members. The hut was not more luxurious or larger than
Aelana and her own. The roof was just high enough to stand up right for Malina, on
the ground were some animal pelts and fur for sleeping. On the far back of the hut,
leaning on some thick branches, which worked as support for the leave roof and
sides, the tablets sat.
On grey slate symbols were engraved. The tablets themselves as long as Malina's arm
and as wide as her forearm. Three tablets filled to the brim with writing. A form of
writing so familiar to her but so different and alien at the same time. She saw the
symbol for tree, but it wasn't the same as she learned it. The lines didn't match up,
there was another dot on top and two new lines going right through. This wasn't
written like Malina herself would write, but it looked so smiliar.
On the third and last tablet, it wasn't a symbol that was engraved at the top, it was a
picture. Something Malina had never seen but knew what it was at first sight. A
Sinbu, one of the forest's giant cats.
