Chapter 1


"Stop pacing," Clair exclaimed. "You are starting to make even me nervous now." Normally, the
Professor didn't get nervous at all. There simply wasn't a situation in which he found himself
uncomfortable or not in control. And even if such a situation would arise every now and then, he
was delighted instead of being afraid or nervous. Being confronted with the unknown or mistakes
was the greatest way to learn something new, that was what the Professor picked up early in his life.
But right now, standing in front of the giant double wooden doors ornamented with hundreds of
scientific discoveries telling the story of the legacy of human nature, his heart was pounding hard in
his chest.
"That is honestly the least of my concerns right now," he said with a much harsher overtone than he
wanted to. Was this how people felt all the time in these sort of situations? It could be an interesting
research project for the future, especially when the hearing today would fail. Well, if it failed, the
Professor was sure there wouldn't be much left to research in a year or so.
"Just remember what we talked about," Clair tried again. "Don't let them provoke you. Stay calm
and present the facts. It's not much you are asking them for, but the payoff even with an error
margin for you being wrong would be enormous."
"I am not wrong." And this was the problem. He rarely was, oh so rarely he could even recollect
every single time he was. But this here today, was definitely not one of them.
"Don't be arrogant was on our list as well. You should really listen to my advice more often, you
know?" The Professor stopped pacing and looked down at Clair sitting on the bench in her simple
black dress and delicate black leather gloves. Because this was an official royal building all visitors
were required to affirm to the dress code given by the emperor himself. Women were restricted so
hard it was laughable.
"Your little headpiece thing is croaked," he answered instead. "No man will want you if you keep
taking so little care of yourself."
"If I walk into a room, no man or woman will notice the croaked headpiece next to you. Next to you
I could wear a rice sack and would look better dressed than you. Please but some effort in here
before talking big. Maybe tuck your shirt?"
The Professor looked down and to his surprise not only was his shirt not tucked in and messy, but
also the buttons on his jacket weren't in order. He left out two of them completely. With a scowl
towards her, he started cleaning things up a bit. Tucking the shirt in hastily and buttoning up his
jacket correctly. He almost dropped the heavy satchel on his side. It was the most important item he
had with him. Filled with all the discoveries and proofs he had for the council.
"Professor, is it getting hot in here or is that because of you?" She was holding the back of her hand
at her forehead faking a faint.
"There are some days I regret taking you from the streets, you know?"
"No there aren't."
Before he could react, the doors opened and the announcer walked out into the hallway. His golden
buttons sparkled from the lighting stones hanging from the ceiling to illuminate the hallway. His
hair was combed forward to shroud his bald spots. But he wasn't fooling anyone with it.
"The Highscholars are ready for you now. Please follow me." He eyed Clair with a vile glance, "Is
she joining us in the hearing?" he asked the Professor. The Professor gave Clair an apologetic look.
He hated this, but he wasn't in a position to press the customs and Highscholars at all. He needed to
be solid, and Clair knew.
"No, she won't. Shall we then?"
#
Without another word, the announcer turned around and walked inside. The Professor looked up
one last time at the inscription above the heavy doors. In bold gold letters stood the motto of the
whole scientific establishment: Pursuit of Truth. He has dedicated his life to the pursuit of truth and
never looked back. Everything led up to this point and now he had to do something he hated most.
Kiss the asses of people, who were far inferior to him. Sadly, he needed something from them.
Something so important every scholar was desperate to obtain. Money.
When the Professor entered the room he was greeted with a wave of silence. All chatter ceased,
heads turned around to see who had entered. Like little sheep who couldn't think for themselves.
"Your next candidate is Cy, Professor for Physics at the Royal University. He issued a grant for
medical research." He turned towards the Professor. "You have ten minutes to argument for your
research and answer the questions of the Highscholars."
"Thank you", he said and bowed his head slightly as was custom. He walked into the middle of the
room, where light would shine through the ceiling windows onto him. The Light of Truth as they
would call it. Utter bullshit. In front of him were seven elderly men in long robes and mostly gray
and white hair. These were the Highscholars, people the Professor hated profusely. They were too
ancient to do anything useful anymore, so they were relegated to decide over money affairs. A job
for children. Behind him a crowd of other scholars, students or bureaucrats sat on benches watching
the spectacle like it was some form of entertainment. It baffled him how nobody seemed to be doing
any significant work around here. What a waste of resources.
"I am honored to speak in front of you today", he said even though the words tasted like vile poison
in his mouth. "I have come to you with a simple, but horrifying message. A message I believe must
be heard, understood and result in action. The plague has crushed our nation and cost us almost a
third of our population. Luckily, with the antidote I found a couple of months ago we could-"
"The antidote you and the council found, you mean?" The Professor bit down so hard he could hear
his teeth crunch. Highscholar Soruma. The worst leech of them all. A man so stuck up and arrogant,
he out shined even the Professor. He would do everything in his power to expose the Professor in
front of everybody here. Soruma was not only a rat, he looked like it too. Or more like a weasel?
Besides his ugly face one could always distinguish him from the other Highscholars by the giant
round lightning infused sapphire around his neck.
"Yes", he said slowly. "The antidote the council and I developed. Even after that, I continued my
research into the virus that caused the plague and found something even more terrifying." The
Professor left a short pause here for dramatic effect, but he soon understood his error. Soruma
would catch onto every little pause to interrupt his speech.
"So let me summarize this really quickly", Soruma began, "this is where the rest of the money went
after we found the antidote? More research of something we already know we found a working
cure? And now you come here before us begging for more money to do what exactly?" Silent
chatter behind the Professor. The sheep were eating it up. He was starting to sweat now. But this is
how they both knew it would go. This is what they planned for.
"You are correct, Highscholar Soruma." Stay calm, he told himself. He had to say these words even
though they went against everything he stood for. Truth. "I spent the rest of that money to study the
virus closer, because I had a gut feeling. Something we all can agree on, is something we can't
ignore as truth-seekers." This time he earned some nods from the other Highscholars and a scowl
from Soruma. "But my findings are worth every gold piece invested. The antidote isn't perfect, it
doesn't kill the virus completely. It leaves traces of it behind, but not only that. It changes the virus
somehow. I can't say how or why this happens, but it does. Therefore I predict another outbreak is
imminent in the next half-year."
The Highscholars were glancing at each other, discussing the impact of his words. They didn't seem
as shocked as he thought they might be. The crowd behind him was talking furiously, but the chatter
died down quickly when Soruma raised his hand to call for silence. The Professor looked at Soruma
and saw a little smile on his face. His body seemed to grow heavier. What was he planning? Why
did he know? And why wasn't he concerned at all? His glance wandered over the rest of the bunch
of pretenders. All wore heavy lightning jewelry, from earrings to necklaces and rings. The bigger
the lightning infused sapphire the bigger they cocks seem to grow looking at them. The Professor
didn't wear any jewelry, but a small perfect cube sapphire around his neck. It was hidden by cloth at
all time and wasn't for public display, but for himself.
"Rumors, rumors, rumors", Soruma exclaimed overly theatrically. "We already know about your,
how did you call it, research." He said the word like it was an insult.
The Professor felt it run cold over his shoulder. "You were spying on me?" he asked with a coarse
voice. He couldn't believe it. What did they know? He wasn't being very careful lately and made too
many mistakes. Mistakes even these old geezers could find out if they only had some luck on their
side.
"After the money we gave you for the antidote vanished, we had some concerns. And so as our right
we looked into the matter. No need to worry though, your secrets are safe with the council of
course." A sly smile. "But what we did find out is, how your work was highly impaired when you
invited that woman into your home. We all thought it was some sort of mistress or wife you bought
yourself, but when we found out you wanted her as your apprentice, we were shocked. We all know
in thousands of years no woman had ever any significant scientific discovery or research. Woman
aren't made to be scholars. And you have the audacity to bring in street filth into our profession. You
disgust-"
"Shut your mouth, or I will swear to all the Gods I will ram my fist up your ass until I hit the empty
shell you call your head." The Professor was shaking now, fists clenched and shouting through his
teeth. The lightning cube around his neck was pulsating fast, it felt the rage inside him as the
lightning always does. "Clair is smarter than all of you combined even without a fancy school or
riches. You should do well not to run your mouth without knowledge." He regretted the words as
soon as they left his mouth, but there wasn't anything he could do about it. He knew it was a trap
and everything fell into place. The Professor wouldn't get out of this with any money and he knew it
as soon as Soruma spoke those words. He would need to find a different way to get that money or
do it without any at all. But he would rather see the whole nation collapse and burn before giving
Soruma the satisfaction. He reseted his right hand on his satchel. It seemed the content wouldn't be
needed today.
"Well, my honored colleagues", Soruma said with a winning grin, "it's clear to see now what we are
dealing with. He has lost his mind completely. How can we trust someone like you, Cy, when all
you give us is insults of our person and our whole profession and world view. You want to compare
a street whore to the council of Highscholars? What is next? She is better suited to run the country
than our Emperor? Even though it's more than you deserve I give you my thanks in the name of the
council for helping with the antidote and your name will be remember for that. But we also have to
look at the facts. I presume it was mere luck that drew you so close to the solution of the problem.
Luck is not a constant we can rely on in your studies. Therefore I want to give this up to a vote now.
Is there anything to add, honored Highscholars?" Soruma looked at the others sitting on their golden
asses nodding at him. They even lost their ability to think for themselves. Eating everything up
Soruma put in front of them. This wasn't a council. But the Professor was calm now. No rage, no
anxiety anymore. He knew exactly what would happen before it did. He looked towards Arth, the
oldest of the Highscholars seated at the very left edge of the table.
Arth cleared his throat, "I would like to add to the thought process, that Cy's research impacted the
future of all our lives. We should not leave this out of the discussion when we decide. Therefore I
will vote for the grant, even though I find this display today despicable and highly inappropriate."
Oh dear old Arth. They call him the eagle. His thick glasses make his eyes appear bulgy. He is the
sole reason they have glasses, which can me customized to every person's own needs. A marvelous
discovery and fascinating process. He found out that with a couple of lightning sapphires and
rotation one can alter glass in all sorts of ways. The technology was forty or so years old by now
and the Professor could still remember picking up the book when he was seventeen and recreating
the process in the basement of his school. It made him smile.
The Highscholars had been voting while he was dreaming about the past, but it didn't matter. Six
vetoes were crushing his hopes of getting the money he needed. The pity vote Without a bow, nod
or word, the Professor left the hall of the council. The announcer proclaimed the hearing to be over,
but he didn't hear a single word of it. Because of personal rivalries and greed, they had doomed the
whole nation.
#
Clair was sitting rather patiently on the little bench outside the hearing room. A group of scholars
walked by shouting her puzzled and vile glances. She could feel her blood begin to boil. Clenching
a fist she put all her rage and hate into her glance. The three men, all probably around thirty to forty
years old, avoided her eyes immediately. That was their biggest problem: on the surface they could
show their convictions against women in science, but deep down they were scared little boys with
no back bone.
There was also a silver lining to this though. The warm and fuzzy feeling of hate and anger in her
belly was something Clair treasured and welcomed. It had been hard work for the last year she had
been with the Professor, that her feeling were slowly coming back to her. Of course, not all has
come back, there were still so much she was keeping bottled inside. But anger was an easy one to
remember for her. There was much to be angry about for Clair.
A silent mutter crept through the cracks underneath the heavy doors. Something happened inside.
Hopefully something good. Clair was fiddling with her dress, trying to flatten her dress over and
over again. He was inside there because of her, she knew it and he knew it. If it wasn't for her, there
would have been no problem. But having a female apprentice was something the Highscholars
couldn't tolerate. Let alone an apprentice like Clair. It just had to work. They needed to say yes.
With all the evidence they meticulously collected over months of intense work they couldn't close
their eyes any longer. The plague was something far greater than this childish dispute over
formalities.
Clair's thoughts were cut short when the door swung open and a stern looking Professor marched
out past her without slowing down. Quickly she jumped up from the bench and followed him
towards the exit. She could feel her stomach twisting together like a vortex. Sweat was creeping out
of her pores and she felt the breakfast toying in her insides.
"What happened?" she asked.
"I hope that was a rhetorical question. I taught you better than that."
Clair looked down to the ground frantically trying to keep up with his large steps. His cold words,
the hint of anger in his voice and indifferent posture was bringing back memories, which were
beginning to overwhelm her. She knew it was her fault, but what could she do about it?
"I am sorry", she whispered.
"Don't apologize for those stuck up bastards. They don't deserve any of it."
"I am not apologizing for them", she said feeling the tears smearing her vision of the white marble
floor. "I know it's my fault we didn't get the grant and you have so many problems with the council
and I know it looks really bad right now, but I will find a way to get us the money for research. Or I
will find a different way of organizing us time and equipment. I just want you to know-"
The Professor stopped abruptly and turned towards her. She wiped her eyes with her leather gloves
and saw him looking down at her. Next to him she always felt like a child, but almost everybody
was dwarfed by his height.
"We lost the grant today because of you", he said, but his voice was warm and calm. Soruma knew
you are my weakness and he exploited it. Calling you names and trying to discredit my mental state
because I took you as an apprentice. He pressed so I pressed back."
"Oh no, what did you say?"
"Nothing too bad, trust me. I had myself well under control."
"Sadly that doesn't mean much with you."
"Well, everybody has a reputation to uphold, doesn't he?" He held out his hands and took Clair by
the shoulders. A gentle but firm grip at the same time. "You know I hate to repeat myself, but it
seems necessary with you a lot. If you hadn't come into my life, they would have found another way
to discredit me and refuse the grant. Soruma and me go way back and it's not about you even
though you might like that. After all the sun doesn't revolve around you."
"Neither does it revolve around the earth or so I heard someone say the other day."
"What a brilliant thought. I would like to meet this mysterious person for a cup of coffee to be
honest with you."
Clair could feel her body relaxing bit by bit. "Well, we shouldn't be talking about these kind of
things here. They hate us enough already we don't want to completely shatter their world view as
well, do we?" Clair smirked.
The Professor smiled. "I like you way better this way, you know. Don't worry so much about these
things. You have the smartest mentor in the world, we will find a way." He started walking again.
"You know I didn't worry about that so much as about.."
"I know", the Professor interrupted. "It saddens me that you have so little trust in me still. I hope I
have given you zero reason to doubt my intentions, but I understand this isn't enough yet. What
would I even do without you all day? Scheme more pranks on my colleagues? Talk with my
research animals. You might not get it, but the reality is, I need you more than you need me." He
laughed. "Isn't that utterly pathetic?"
Clair smiled. "Yes, we truly are." The guards opened the doors to the outside when they approached.
Warm spring sun greeted them on top of the stairs leading down to the main roads. The colored
roofs seem to shine like little colorful suns. Warm reds, oranges and yellows paired with sprinkles
of blue and green. This was one of the richest districts in Shima, the capital, far away from the filthy
harbors, where Clair grew up. In the distance dark clouds were forming. An imminent thunderstorm
was approaching and Clair made a mental note to record the time, intensity and duration of that
thunderstorm. It was one of the Professors favorite projects to research and it was her turn this week
to record the data. Somehow he saw a pattern in the occurrence of the storms, like they weren't
natural.
Walking down the steps she noticed a royal carriage at the bottom of the stairs. Only high ranked
officials of the Emperor were given the authority to use these carriages. They were made from light
birch wood, polished and embellished with carvings of mountains, trees and animals found further
north, where the Emperor and his family came from. The gold linings was reflecting the sunlight.
But Clair wasn't impressed by the artistic display of wealth, she was feeling bad for the four men,
who needed to pull the carriage up and down the hills of Shima. Horses were too expensive and
wild for such a task, even though all sorts of scholars had tried to breed better suited horses, or so
the Professor had told her once.
An immaculately dressed woman was standing in front of the open carriage door. Her black hair
was combed back into a long, shiny ponytail and she watched her and the Professor with
penetrating dark eyes. The insignia of the Emperor, a white wolf, triumphantly was embroidered on
her chest.
"Do you think she wants something from us?" the Professor asked with curiosity. Clair could see his
eyes wander up and down. He had this weird thing for powerful women, she had noticed. Maybe
powerful was the wrong word, but demanding would do it more justice.
"I don't think she would ever want anything from you. I mean look at her and then look in the
mirror for once, do you see what I mean?"
She earned a scowl from the Professor, giggling into her leather gloves.
"Greetings, Cy and Clair", the messenger woman exclaimed. "I am Sura Carin of the Royal
Treasury. Can you spare some time and have a chat with me?" The Royal Treasure? This has to be
about the money, which disappeared. Clair began to sweat again. Her legs felt like noodles. There
didn't seem enough air around her anymore.
"So she does want something from us", the Professor said with a triumphant smile. "I guess
someone was wrong again."
He looked at her knowingly. Does he not understand what this means? she thought. "Please
Professor, this seems highly inappropriate, don't you think?" Clair said and felt her cheeks blush. He
felt no shame, indifferent to anyone he was speaking to. A royal messenger was not someone you
disrespected. Especially not in their position.
"Apologies. We would be delighted to hear your mysterious offer." He winked at Clair.
"Splendidly", Sura gestured them into the little carriage and Clair followed the Professor inside,
sitting down next to him on the perfectly white and comfy cushioned seats. The inside was quite
simple and not as pompous as the outside, but still it was screaming wealth and money. How many
days would you be able to feed a family with the money that went into this vehicle, Clair thought.
The small enclosure made her feel a bit nauseous, the air was stuffy and thick and she began to play
with the folds of her dress again.
"As you know, the Emperor is very concerned about the plague and its repercussions on the capital
and whole nation. He isn't taking this as lightly as some other people."
"You mean the council of Highscholars, perhaps?" the Professor asked. Why can't he just shut up
and not make her angry before anything has started? Her heart was pounding fast and her fingers
were slippery.
Sura looked at the Professor for a moment and folded her hands on top of her thighs, "You might be
accustomed to talking over woman in your profession, Professor, but as I can assume you being
above average intelligence you know how to treat a member of the royal court. I suggest you
remember what you learned and not behave like a little child in front of a person, who is still willing
to help you. Did I make myself clear?" So composed, so clear. She reminded Clair weirdly enough
of the Professor himself.
The Professor looked taken aback, "I meant no disrespect with my interruption. It will not happen
again, I will assure you, Sura Carin." He bowed his head slightly. This wasn't like him at all. What
is going on? The fact that she didn't understand anything was making her even more nervous. The
Professor on the other hand was oozing out confidence and calmness.
"So, we are interested in making your research project a reality. We are concerned, even if some
have spoken against, that your research might have some valid reasoning and we are looking at
another outbreak. Therefore the Royal Treasure is offering you a somewhat different option than a
normal grant. Are you following?"
Clair was nodding even though she knew Sura was barely talking at her. She was there because it
would have been impolite to not invite her inside the carriage as well. But this woman demanded a
reaction.
"Do you allow me a quick question?" the Professor inquired.
Sura nodded, but her eyebrows went up. Don't push her, Clair begged. "Why do you offer me even
more money for my research? Don't you think the grant from the council is enough?"
Sura's face stayed a perfect mask, but her eyes gave out a hint of fear. Clair grew more and more
interested in what the Professor had in mind. This wasn't going in the direction she thought it would
go.
Sura opened her mouth, but nothing came out. Silence covered the carriage. Clair didn't dare to look
towards the Professor, but had her eyes glued on the royal messenger. "I was under the impression
the grant was denied for you", she finally said.
"Yes, we were heartbroken when the judgment was called, weren't we Clair?" He gave no time for a
response. "But I guess something is quite puzzling for me. Maybe you can spread some light on the
matter at hand: how does a royal messenger know the outcome of a council hearing before it was
concluded? I mean I didn't see anybody race me to the exit doors informing you of the outcome, did
you Clair?" Again, no pause. "And then there is the problem of you already being here waiting for
us. It seems to me rather strange. Can you help me out with this little puzzle?"
Clair began to understand. But still, it didn't make any sense. Somehow the Professor knew the
answer already.
The messenger woman tried to escape his eyes, but the pause made her even more uncomfortable.
"I normally don't get into these sort of situations, I have to apologize for my lack of
professionalism. You are correct, I knew the outcome before the hearing had taken place."
"Unsurprising really. So what is this different option, as you put it."
"Alright", Sura began finding her composure again, "we are under the impression for the highest
chance of success we need to diversify our research options. Not only will we fund projects in all
five kingdoms, but also outside. You are in the unique position, that you have no backing of the
council or any of your colleagues for that matter. You won't be much use of us here, if you can't
work with other scholars. But there is another branch we like to follow."
"Enlighten us, please."
"The Maru grasslands have a broad and diverse plant life. We have very little data on any of their
medicinal capacities and with the new virus we need to look into all option-"
She was interrupted by loud laughter from the Professor. It wasn't even his fake laughter to annoy or
belittle, he was actually amused. Whenever he was enjoying himself he would laugh with an open
mouth and show much of his teeth, but also he would be aware of that and trying to hide his big
mouth with his palm.
"What do you think, Clair", he said after he calmed down a bit, "would you like to travel the
grasslands to study some plants? Does that sound like a great time to you? What could go wrong,
right?"
"I have to admit", Clair said trying to sound as confident as the Professor, "it sounds not only stupid
but reckless. Who came up with this ridiculous plan?"
"It doesn't matter you came up with it. What matters is the future. And I include your own future
here as well." Something in her tone did scare Clair. "I want to be honest with you, especially after
seeing you are not easily fooled. This is not really an offer than an order. I don't want to waste my
or your time any longer."
"What happens if I refuse?" the Professor asked now without any emotion. "I have a job and other
research avenues to follow. Why would I endanger my life with this project?"
"There has been rising pressure from the council to remove you from the royal university. The
students seem to have found a liking towards you and their parents are inclined to keep you in your
current position. But they aren't as powerful as they once were. Your support is dwindling and with
some intervention from higher up.. We of course don't want to go there. It would be bad for both
sides, but as you might see it would be fatal for you." No, this was impossible.
"You can't do that", Clair said ignoring the knot in her stomach telling her to keep quiet.
"It's alright, Clair. Let her speak."
"You will have a week to make arrangements and will leave with a trade caravan making a round
trip through the grasslands. You will be disguised as merchants interested in new herbs and
medicine. Try to make contact with healers of the different tribes and learn as much as possible."
"When will I be allowed to return?" The question made her heart freeze.
"As soon as you find a promising candidate or when the half-year prognosis of yours ends. After
that we will have certainty either way."
"Was that all then?"
The messenger nodded. "You may leave now."
The Professor left the carriage quickly and Clair followed. As soon as the door closed behind them,
the carriage went off into the distance. The warm sun couldn't shake the shiver Clair felt through her
whole body.
"They can't do this, can they?" she asked.
"Don't embarrass yourself and think before you speak. It is all very clear."
"It is clear?" She was getting angry now. "How can you say this. They want to ship you off into the
grasslands and you will just stand there and take it? I need you here." The tears were starting again.
"I have no choice, but to follow. But you have a choice, Clair. You have a week to decide."
"You know how I am going to decide. I can't leave San behind. I am all he has got left."
He lay his hand on her shoulder, gentle but firm. "I know."
Nothing more needed to be said. The man he thought of as her father was leaving her. And again
she was powerless. She didn't feel sad, all she felt was rage.
