Chapter 7


"What do you mean thirty?" Clair asked thunderstruck. The Professor was walking up and down
trying to think of something. He was talking with himself like he always did.
"Oh, maybe I can find some more", Calandi said. "There must be some more somewhere." She left
the bag on the ground and fled. The dark clouds were coming dangerously close. White lightning
and deep thunder. Even the air felt already charged.
"What was your plan anyway?" Clair asked the Professor.
"Doesn't really matter, does it now? I was experimenting with sapphires a couple of years ago, but
put it on ice because it was boring me. I wanted to get closer to the nature of the lighting, but all I
found was a way to extend the lifespan of a charged sapphire. Useful, but not very interesting, so I
left it behind. But we could have used some of the techniques to create a shield above us. Sapphire
attract lightning like nothing else, so if we had enough of them we could make a grid above our
heads and hope they are enough to redirect all the lightning away from us. But with so few, there is
no way we can protect everybody, especially not the animals. I am quite afraid to see what will
happen if the others hear of that, that there is only a small safe space for all of us. I don't think it
will be a healthy in environment to be in."
Clair was thinking, but nothing quite made sense. She wasn't well versed in lightning theories and
she wasn't an engineer. If she was honest, she wasn't anything at all. She had some separate talents
here and there, but nothing really useful. "So, we need to create a shield above us with about fifteen
sapphire to redirect the lightning away from us. What if we could move the sapphires around?
Would that cover more area then?"
The Professor stopped, "It might. But how are you gonna move them? They would have to be
moving quite fast to have the effect we want. Nothing we can do with our weak bodies." And he
started pacing again.
"How about human sacrifice? Maybe the Gods will be pleased with us then and let us survive."
"If the Gods actually cared about any of us, they wouldn't send us plagues, which kill a third of the
population, would they?"
No time for bad jokes then. "Cy?" she asked slowly. He stopped in his tracks again and looked at
her confused. "Are we going to die?" It was a simple question. She wasn't particularly anxious
about it, she often had wished it to happen actually. Now that it was imminent it felt different, but
she was mostly concerned about the Professor and a little about Calandi as well. There were
probably more good hearted people in this group she didn't even know of. They didn't want to die
and they didn't deserve to die.
"Clair", he said slowly with a warm smile, "I promise you, we will not die here. You haven't fought
your way through the life you had and end up dead by a thunderstorm out in no-mans-land. Our
story won't end here, it has just began. I mean who else is gonna save the nation from a civilization
killing plague?"
"How can you be this optimistic in our situation? I can see you are struggling to get an answer to
our problem, because there really is nothing we can do anymore. How can you promise something
like this? Empty promises are worse than the truth. Just say it out loud, that we are going to die out
here and don't give me some heartwarming promise, we both now is just crap. You can't do anything
about this storm."
"That's precisely where you are wrong, there is always something you can do. It's especially in these
moments, when it seems impossible, we become something greater. The void won't beat you just
because it sucks you in. It..", he trailed off.
"It's not like you to get that philosophical or even stop mid lecture. Please tell me, you are having a
stroke because your words make me want to vomit."
"Negative charge", he exclaimed ignoring Clair completely. "So simple, so elegant. Don't you see?"
"I see how imminent death is making you even more self centered."
"I listened to every word you said, but none of them deserve any answer, so I won't bother. You can
keep talking shit at me or help me so we can stick it to Soruma in the end."
Clair breathed in deep, "So what is negative charge? A sapphire is either charged or not. Should I
have been paying more attention while you were lecturing me on this stuff?"
The Professor scowled at her quickly and walked over to one of the wagons. Clair followed. "You
are right, a sapphire is either charged or it isn't. Quite simple. Every farmer knows this. Put a
charged sapphire in a lantern and you have light for a few days or weeks, depending on the quality
and size of the gem. But", he stopped for a moment going through all sorts of apparatuses
underneath the tarp, "are you familiar with how glasses are made these days?"
"Sure, I run one of the most prominent glass manufacturing businesses in all of Shima. I just hang
out at your place because of the great company and delicious food."
The Professor surfaced a heavy box shaped.. thing? She wasn't sure what it actually was. It had lots
of mirrors, glass tubes, maybe some lenses or were they something else? Lots of gears and cables
connecting different parts of the box. It looked so foreign, she actually had never seen it anywhere
in the Professor's place. Normally he was quite proud of his experiments and gadgets and was
happy to show them to Clair, of course always with the thought of teaching her something. But this
abomination she had never seen before. "There we go", he said proudly. "Good that I actually
brought you with me my love." He had obvious problems with the weight of the contraption, even
though he had a quite muscular stature for a scholar.
"So, what is this?" she asked. Her curiosity was sparked, something the Professor had hidden even
from her.
"When our good friend Arth, the Highscholar, started to research charged sapphires, he found a way
to change the nature of glass with the help of some good old fashioned luck and experiment. The
glass was after being processed by the machine easier to make into lenses for seeing aid. It made
him so much money he basically stopped researching entirely and spent his time with something
else, you know what. But I was fascinated by the notion to use the power of charged sapphires
without a Charged taking control of the power. And this is what I came up with." He pointed at the
contraption on the ground, but then went over tot he wagon to grab some other things. "So it uses
some interesting tricks to discharge the sapphires and focus their energy on a target. His version of
this only focused the energy on a surface maybe the size of your chest. I have been able to
concentrate on the surface of a thumbnail."
"Why would you want that?"
"Well, for example it can cut through solid iron. I call it The Lightning Cutter. The name is still a
work in progress though."
Clair's eyes widened, "It can cut through iron? You are kidding, right?"
"I wish I was, but it's true."
"You make it sound like it's something bad. This could revolutionize everything. If we can use the
power of the storms in a way like this, we can make the lives of so many people easier.
Manufacturing of everything made from metals will change fundamentally."
"Quite romantic thoughts you have there", he said while taking out another round metal ball with
lots of openings and iron rods. "But let's not talk about that now, we need to actually make this work
before the storm front hits or we won't be able to have a conversation about this anymore. Grab
some of the stuff and get it over into the middle of the circle."
"So, I know there are more pressing matters at hand, but just one question: why the actual fuck did
you bring a contraption like this with you? Not only does it take up enormous space and weight, but
in a week or so you won't be able to use it at all. All sapphires will have lost their charge."
The Professor sighed, "You might see good things resulting from it, and I wish for those things as
well. They are the reasons I was looking into it in the first place. But the reality is, that this thing
would see most use in warfare rather than in manufacturing. It's a weapon, which might be able to
puncture a Charged lightning cloak. What do you think it will do to normal soldiers on the front?"
Clair hadn't thought about that possibility at all. Why was she always so naive. It was so obvious
this thing would be used to kill people rather than make their lives easier. "And I couldn't leave it
back home", he said and heaved the thing up from the ground again. Clair was grabbing some of the
other supplies. "Soruma has probably been going through my stuff like a little snooping rat since we
left. He will use the things I have achieved and put his name on it to further his reputation and
diminish my own, but I don't care very much about that anymore. But I can't let him have this little
baby, and I couldn't bring myself to destroy it. So I brought it with us, lucky for us, really."
"Let's just wait if it works or it burns us all alive."
"I always thought I would die from one of my inventions."
"Me too", Clair laughed and the Professor joined in. The thunder in the background was getting
quite loud now and the air was feeling electric. The other men from the caravan were still busy
carrying all material made from iron outside the circle of the wagons. The animals were getting
uneasy and Clair felt bad for them, they had no idea what was going on. They were probably even
more frightened than the people around them.
Calandi came running when she saw them enter the barricaded circle, "What are you two carrying
there? I thought we should put all the metal outside the circle? What is going on? Will it work, Cy?
Can we actually survive this? Maybe we should have tried to get to the military outpost. A few
made their way there, but maybe we should have pressed harder on that. And now we are all stuck
here and.."
"Breathe Calandi", the Professor said calmly. His voice was so steady and soothing. "Everything is
going to be alright. It might get a little rocky and the animals will get spooked, but I promise we
have made the correct decision in staying here." Well, at least he didn't promise her they would all
survive, Clair thought.
"But what-"
"Please Calandi", he interrupted, "let us work this out. I need you to keep everything else busy
doing something else and not get into our way. And try to calm the animals. I don't want to get
trampled by one of those bulls when the storm hits. Can you do that for me?" She nodded. "Alright.
Everything will be alright."
Calandi scuffled off and Clair shouted after her, "Give the bulls some food and make someone
brush them. It tends to calm them." She nodded again and was off trying to give orders to the
frightened men.
All of her muscles tensed up when she saw Deko walk over to them with a cigar in the corner of his
mouth. She always hated the smell and taste of smoke, especially cigars. It made her feel like
vomiting only seeing the swirls of smoke coming from the ignited end. "What are you two up to?"
he asked with his deep, dark voice.
"Nothing that concerns you, Deko", Clair spat out. "But I see you are too busy to help around here,
so do us all a favor and be useless over there."
"Feisty, isn't she today professor?" he laughed.
The Professor didn't look up from his work, from where Clair was standing it looked like he was
getting the few sapphires they had collected from everybody into sockets of the round metal ball. "I
have no desire to engage in conversation with you. I would appreciate if you take Clair's suggestion
though."
Deko ignored them both and looked intensely at the contraption. "Interesting little bit of junk you
got there. What does it do?" He exhaled another cloud of smoke, luckily the wind was blowing it
away from Clair.
"Piece of junk?" the Professor asked flabbergasted and stopped what he was doing. "The only piece
of junk is your pathetic ass snooping around in our business. You either do something productive
with your time or shut the fuck up and let me concentrate. This contraption will either save or kill us
all so please let me see to it, that it won't be the latter."
Deko stepped a bit back, held out his hands in defense, but didn't say another word. The Professor
mounted his Lightning Cutter on a tripod pointing with one end to the ground, hopefully where the
energy would be directed to, and the other end straight up in the air. They then connected the round
ball to the contraption and built a little pole for it to be as high up as possible. "I would like it to be
a little higher, but this all we can get right now I suppose", the Professor said stepping back from
their little project. It looked quite pathetic. How can something like this save them from the
thunderstorm, Clair asked herself.
"How far do you think it will keep us safe?" she asked him.
"No idea. We will have to see when it all starts. The only problem now is turning it on. If we turn it
on too early, all the little energy we have in the sapphires will not keep the machine running for
long enough to let the storm fuel it. And if we do it too late, we are dead."
"Great", she said dryly.
"Stiffen up that upper lip, little lady", he said and smiled. "This will work out just fine, you'll see.
But now we wait."

An uneasy group of men and women were standing around the little iron pole the Professor and
Clair had built. Nobody was in the mood for talking much, and if so only in hushed voices or so
they sounded to Clair over the loud thunder. Some fell back to prayer and seeing if that will have
any impact, quite doubtful, Clair thought. She was running her finger through the short fur of one of
the bulls. The air felt electric already and it was getting chilly, but with the warmth of the trusty bull
she felt alright. The animals were getting quite uneasy and Clair had told them to round them up
into one big group so they could help each other. She had no idea if it was an actually good idea or
would make things even worse. She never had farm animals or worked on a farm. The concept was
even more foreign to her than the contraption the Professor had built in the middle of their
makeshift camp.
The storm didn't care about any of this. It rolled forward over the land, unstoppable, unapologetic,
untouchable and without purpose. So carefree, she almost envied it.
"Shh", she whispered in the bulls ears, "it's going to be alright. The professor built something really
nice, that is going to protect us from the lightning. It will only be quite loud, but that's all it's going
to be. Nothing but a little noise. Close your eyes and imagine you are somewhere else, anywhere
you want. Maybe a far reaching pasture with lots of delicious grass?" The bull didn't answer. "Well,
maybe something else then. A barn filled to the brim with hay, all your friends with you. In safety,
closed off from the storm." Maybe she was imagining it, but she felt like the bull nodded slightly.
"Where would I want to be?" she whispered to nobody at all.
The thunder was strenuous on the ears by now, hundreds of lightnings striking the ground and
vanishing where they hit. It was still a mystery why the lightning strikes wouldn't have any effect on
inanimate things. Rock, sand or even trees would stay mostly unharmed from the white bolts, but
people and animals were burned heavily or simply turned to ash after a few bolts hit their bodies.
The rain front hit them first, everything was dry a moment ago and then like a curtain of water
swiped over them carried by strong winds. The storm had reached them, turning the light of day
into the dark gray of dusk. Soon the onslaught of lightning bolts would make it as bright as day
again. She looked over to the Professor, who nodded at her staying ready for the perfect moment to
turn on the machine. He will find the right timing, she though.
The bulls were getting antsy and Clair tried walking between them, brushing their warm bodies with
her hands. Sometimes she bent down and shouted something in their ears, but they probably didn't
even know she was saying anything. The noise was so loud her ears actually start hurting. She tried
to look towards the east, but the wagons were in the way. The lightning front was close now, she
was sure of it.
Looking around she found Deko standing as close to the metal pole as possible. Of course, he had
claimed one of the safest places around here. So they wouldn't get lucky and have him get struck by
lightning. When he would get struck so close to the pole, they were all at risk of dying, so she didn't
want to think about that. On the other hand, she thought, ridding the world of a slime like Deko was
worth dying probably.
Then a blue light shone from the little metal ball, they had put the charged sapphires in. Little
sparks flew around the poles and cables underneath and at the bottom a pale light started to emerge.
She couldn't see if it did anything to the ground, but it looked quite underwhelming. And this was
suppose to be used in warfare, she questioned. Maybe the Professor had exaggerated one of his
inventions again. It was something that happened quite frequently actually. But he looked satisfied
with his invention and was watching it with intense interest, as did Deko for some reason. He was
showing an odd interest in this machine, almost like he was studying it.
The lightning front hit them merciless. The wind picked up even more and Clair's short hair stuck to
her head like a wet second skin. The rain had completely drenched her. Lightning bolts suspended
the darkness that had began to shroud them. First it were only a couple hitting the ground around
them, some even hitting the wagons but dispersing into the ground without any reaction. As
predicted, most of them hit the little metal ball filled with sapphires powering the Cutter. More and
more lightning bolts rained down and instead of coming down in zig zag lines, they all bent towards
the metal ball, like they were sucked into the machine. First only the ones in the immediate vicinity,
but then more and more joined them. It looked like they were pulled into the contraption from the
skies themselves. The bolts were getting so close to each other, the light was blinding to the eyes
and everybody had to avert their eyes. It was spectacular. Even more so, the beam coming from the
machine, was getting just as white and blinding as the lightnings flooding in from the top. Like
some invisible shield over them, no bolt reached the ground around the pole.
Clair started laughing and locked eyes with the Professor, who was ecstatic. He was probably
making notes in his head right now, ready to transcribe them as soon as the rain was going to stop.
He was harnessing the power of a thunderstorm without being a Charged. What else could the
lightning be used for? she asked herself.
The machine was giving out high-pitched crackles every now and then. Out of the corner of her eye
she saw a big shape move suddenly. She turned towards it and saw the bulls getting anxious. The
sounds were making them more nervous than the lightning. One of them had broke lose from the
rest of the herd. Clair couldn't hear if he was making any sounds, but he looked like he wanted to
run. Not only would that mean certain death to the creature, but he could also take some of the
travelers with him. Clair dashed over to him, the rain hitting her in the face and making it hard to
see anything. The wind was creeping in through her wet clothes.
The bull was shaking his head from side to side with no real direction to it. He seemed incredibly
anxious and even shivered when she put her hand on his back. Underneath the wet fur, he was warm
and his muscles felt tense. She tried to rub the part of his forehead, like she did with all of them. He
didn't calm down though. She looked around and only saw the other people clear a path around the
bull, ready for it to run rampant away from them. The machine was giving them a greater area of
safety than she had anticipated, but this still wouldn't save the bull. There must be something she
could do for him.
Looking for the Professor she understood quickly that he was absorbed in thought, watching his
little miracle unfold in front of him. She tried to shout, but he probably wouldn't have heard it even
he stood right next to her. So she put the head of the bull against her own head watching deep into
the big, dark eyes of him. She grabbed the head as tight as she could, giving the animal no room to
sway from left to right like he had done before. It worked for a moment, but then a tarp from one of
the wagons was blown lose by the strong wind and hit the two from the side. The sudden impact
and darkness made the animal lose all control and reason, so it broke out. It happened so quickly,
Clair tried to get away, but she couldn't let go. Not because the tarp was holding her onto the
animal, but because she couldn't let him go. He was frightened, they all were. Nobody cared about
him. It wasn't right. She held on tightly trying to calm it down. With one hand she was holding
herself on the horns of the bull, her body clenching the back of the bull. With the other one she
pried the tarp away, maybe if he saw the light again he would come back to his senses and calm
down.
When the tarp fell off, he wasn't stopping. They were heading towards the line of wagons, she
couldn't do anything now. They would hit the barricade, whatever happened after that was
impossible to foresee. Then the bull shook his head and body so hard, Clair was catapulted off the
animal and landed in the mud, slithering for few meters on the wet ground before finally coming to
a halt. It was like he had wanted to help her and not run her to her death. The bull was still running
straight towards the wagons and Clair tried to reach him with her left arm, but it was of course a
stupid gesture. She couldn't do anything anymore, couldn't reach him. She was shouting for him to
stop without, but the thunder was so loud she couldn't even hear her own words. They were simply
sucked from her mouth when they left her cold lips. All she could do, was watch the tragedy unfold.
Powerless as always. Tears were forming in her eyes.
She felt a firm, but gentle hand land on her shoulder. In the same instance a blinding light hit her
stretched out arm and everything went numb, from her hand down her whole body. Even her heart
seemed to stop beating for the moment. She didn't feel any pain, but she could smell her skin and
flesh burn.
